Business Challenge
World Tracer dependency, mishandling rates, no IATA 753 readiness
▼

Qatar Airways managed baggage across 150+ global stations using World Tracer — a system built on cryptic codes that required extensive staff training and created frequent data-entry errors. Mishandled baggage rates were a persistent customer experience problem, and the airline had no platform to deliver the mandatory IATA Resolution 753 baggage tracking that was coming into force. A purpose-built, operationally intuitive solution was needed — one that agents at outstations could use with minimal training.

👤
My Role
End-to-end ownership — requirements through global rollout
▼

As Manager IT, Ground Services at Qatar Airways, I owned this programme from initiation to full global deployment. This included requirements definition, vendor coordination, UAT, change management, and phased rollout across the network — while simultaneously managing the live baggage IT estate across 150+ airports.

  • Contributed to IATA Resolution 753 as a Baggage Working Group member in 2016 — directly influencing the policy HAQIBA would then implement
  • Defined the functional architecture of HAQIBA — plain language over cryptic codes, DCS-integrated scanning, Oracle Finance connectivity
  • Managed the full global rollout across QR stations, training, and go-live support
⚙
Solution Delivered
Purpose-built BMS replacing World Tracer across the full network
▼
  • HAQIBA (QHOME GUI) replaced World Tracer — intuitive interface with plain-language descriptions, colour-coded class routing (Economy yellow, Business blue, First burgundy)
  • Auto-population of passenger data from Amadeus Altéa DCS on tag scan — eliminated manual re-entry and a key source of matching errors
  • Oracle Finance integration for auditable, end-to-end claims settlement
  • E-mail PIR delivery direct to passengers — replaced paper-based processes network-wide
  • Full IATA Resolution 753 compliance — bag tracked at all 4 mandatory touchpoints across 150+ airports
📈
Business Impact
Industry award, IATA 753 pioneer, mishandling reduction
▼
  • −30% baggage tracing cycle time — faster file creation, automated matching, and picture-based identification dramatically accelerated resolution vs World Tracer
  • −45% reduction in fraudulent claims — Oracle Finance integration, mandatory document uploads, and end-to-end audit trail made fraudulent claims measurably harder to sustain and easier to identify
  • Staff training cut from 6 days to 2 days — plain English interface eliminated code memorisation, reducing onboarding by two-thirds and cutting error rates across all 150+ stations
  • First airline globally achieving full IATA 753 compliance across an entire network — cited as an industry reference implementation
  • Fully paperless operations — eliminated physical files, telex reports, and OCS document shipments, reducing cost and preventing document loss
  • Industry award recognition — Qatar Airways Baggage Management programme recognised externally

⚠
Business Challenge
Passengers requiring document checks forced to abort kiosk and join manual queue
▼

At Qatar Airways, a persistent friction point in kiosk check-in was the handling of passengers whose travel documents — visas, entry permits, travel authorisations — required manual verification. The existing process required these passengers to abort the kiosk journey mid-way, join a separate document-check queue, and be manually checked in at a staffed counter. This created delays, passenger frustration, and operational inefficiency — and undermined the purpose of self-service entirely. At DIAL, the challenge was different but related: kiosks and self-bag drops existed as disconnected touchpoints. Passengers had no designed flow guiding them from kiosk check-in through to bag drop, resulting in underutilisation of both systems and continued queue pressure at staffed counters.

👤
My Role
Solution design, application architecture, and implementation at both Qatar Airways and DIAL
▼

At Qatar Airways, led the design and implementation of the CUSS kiosk check-in application — including the Automated Travel Document Check feature. At DIAL, designed the end-to-end passenger self-service flow, connecting kiosk check-in and self-bag drop into a single, guided passenger journey.

  • Defined the kiosk application functional architecture at Qatar Airways — integrated with Amadeus Altéa DCS for real-time passenger data
  • Designed the Automated Travel Document Check notification workflow — including staff tablet alert system
  • At DIAL, mapped and redesigned the passenger flow from terminal entry through kiosk check-in to self-bag drop, identifying and removing friction points that were suppressing self-service adoption
⚙
Solution Delivered
Automated document check notification · Staff tablet alerts · End-to-end self-service passenger flow
▼
  • Automated Travel Document Check (Qatar Airways): when the kiosk identifies that a passenger's visa or travel document requires manual verification, rather than aborting the check-in, the system automatically alerts the nearest available staff member via their tablet — with passenger details, seat, and document requirement. Staff come to the kiosk, verify the document on-the-spot, and the passenger completes check-in at the kiosk without being redirected to a counter
  • Eliminated the need for passengers requiring document checks to join a separate queue — check-in journey completed at the kiosk in all cases
  • DIAL Passenger Flow Design: redesigned the self-service passenger journey — clear wayfinding, sequenced touchpoints, and process alignment between kiosk check-in and self-bag drop to create a continuous, guided flow
  • Removed the operational disconnect between kiosk and bag drop that was causing passenger drop-off and reverting them to staffed counters
📈
Business Impact
Passengers no longer redirected from kiosk · Queue reduction at DIAL · Higher self-service adoption
▼
  • Passengers requiring travel document verification no longer required to abort kiosk check-in and join a separate manual queue — a significant improvement in passenger experience and operational flow
  • Staff tablet notification meant document verification was handled proactively and quickly, without creating a secondary queue or counter backlog
  • At DIAL, the redesigned kiosk-to-bag-drop passenger flow contributed to a 30% reduction in check-in queues at staffed counters as self-service adoption increased
  • Demonstrated that self-service failure points are often process and flow problems — not technology problems — and that fixing the journey around the technology is as important as the technology itself

⚠
Business Challenge
No real-time airside visibility — fleet idling, fuel waste, manual turnaround coordination
▼

Airside vehicle operations at IGI Delhi had no real-time visibility. Ground support equipment — buses, tugs, belt loaders, fuel vehicles — was dispatched manually with no tracking of location, utilisation, or idle time. This led to vehicles idling in the wrong zones, unnecessary fuel consumption, and coordination delays that affected aircraft turnaround times. There was no data on which vehicles were where, which were idle, or how long turnarounds were actually taking versus planned. Decisions were made on radio calls and experience — not information.

👤
My Role
End-to-end solution design and implementation — IoT tracking and turnaround concept
▼

Led the design and implementation of the IoT-based vehicle tracking system at DIAL — from solution architecture through hardware selection, integration with the Airport Operations Control Centre, and live deployment across the airside fleet. Separately, used the operational data and IoT infrastructure as the foundation for designing a concept Aircraft Turnaround Management solution using IoT devices and Computer Vision.

  • Defined the solution architecture — IoT hardware selection, GPS tracking integration, AOCC dashboard design
  • Managed vendor coordination and deployment across the full airside vehicle fleet
  • Designed the Aircraft Turnaround Management concept — using IoT sensors on ground support equipment and Computer Vision to monitor turnaround milestones in real time, replacing manual radio-based coordination
⚙
Solution Delivered
Live airside fleet tracking · AOCC integration · IoT + CV turnaround concept
▼
  • IoT-based real-time GPS tracking deployed across all airside ground support vehicles — live location, utilisation, and idle-time visibility for the first time
  • AOCC dashboard integration — operations controllers could see the full airside picture without radio calls or manual status updates
  • Smart dispatch — vehicles routed to where they were needed based on real-time position, reducing idle time and unnecessary fuel consumption
  • Aircraft Turnaround Management Concept (IoT + Computer Vision): designed a solution architecture using IoT devices on ground equipment to log turnaround event milestones — boarding bridge connect/disconnect, fuel start/stop, cargo door open/close — combined with Computer Vision cameras positioned at stands to validate and monitor the turnaround sequence automatically, providing airlines and handlers with shared real-time turnaround status without manual reporting
📈
Business Impact
+40% fleet utilisation · −14% fuel · Turnaround visibility transformed
▼
  • Fleet utilisation improved by 40% — vehicles deployed where needed, when needed, with real-time visibility replacing guesswork
  • Fuel consumption reduced by 14% — idle time reduction and optimised routing were the primary drivers
  • Airside coordination moved from reactive radio calls to proactive data-driven dispatch — a fundamental shift in how the operations team worked
  • The Aircraft Turnaround Management concept established a blueprint for using IoT and Computer Vision together to monitor ground operations — a solution that airlines and airports can deploy to improve on-time departure performance without adding manual reporting overhead
  • Demonstrated that operational efficiency gains at airports are often a data problem first — and that IoT provides the data layer that makes everything else possible

AI in Aviation

AI-Driven Disruption Management Is the Most Valuable Use Case in Aviation Right Now

IROPs cost airlines billions annually. The data to fix this already exists — the problem is that the people who have it and the systems that need it are not connected in real time. AI changes that, but only if it is designed by someone who has stood behind the counter at 2 AM and understands what an agent actually needs: not a dashboard, not an alert — the right action, pre-suggested, ready to execute.

Every premium hotel has a concierge. Every private bank has a relationship manager. Airlines have a FAQ page. The mobile app is already in every passenger's hand. The data feeds exist. Passenger Processing

The Future of Passenger Processing Is Not About Technology — It Is About Flow

DigiYatra reached 50,000 journeys in month one — not because the technology was impressive, but because the passenger flow around it was designed correctly. Self-service adoption fails almost always at the process level, not the technology level. The kiosk that works is the one that fits how passengers actually move through a terminal.

A digital twin of airport operations sounds straightforward until you try to build one. The data exists — in AODB, FIDS, BHS, IoT sensors, airline DCS feeds — but it lives in disconnected systems with different owners and different incentives to share. The technology is not the hard part. The governance and integration architecture is.
